## You Just Bought Five Trees. Now What?

You're standing in your driveway with a truck bed full of maples and a shovel in your hand. It's a Saturday in Boise, the sun is out, and you're ready to dig. But here's the question that'll determine whether those trees thrive for decades or struggle through their first summer: **Is today actually the right day to plant?**

Timing matters more than most homeowners realize. The best time to plant trees in Boise isn't when the nursery has its best sale or when you finally get a free weekend. It's when the soil temperature, air temperature, and moisture conditions give your tree the strongest possible start.

We've planted thousands of trees across the Treasure Valley over the past 15 years. We've seen what works. We've seen what doesn't. And we've helped plenty of homeowners replace trees that went in the ground at the wrong time.

Here's what you need to know, straight from the people who do this every day.

## Fall Is King: Why Mid-September Through October Is the Sweet Spot

If you want one clear answer, here it is: **fall is the best time to plant trees in Boise.** Specifically, mid-September through the end of October.

That's not just our opinion. The [International Society of Arboriculture](https://www.treesaregood.org) recommends fall planting in climates like ours, and the [University of Idaho Extension](https://www.uidaho.edu/extension) backs it up with research specific to our region.

Here's why fall wins in the Treasure Valley:

- **Roots keep growing after leaves drop.** When a deciduous tree goes dormant in fall, the top stops demanding energy. But the roots? They keep pushing into the soil as long as ground temperatures stay above 40°F. In Boise, that typically gives you six to eight weeks of root growth before the ground freezes.
- **Less transplant shock.** Cooler air temperatures mean less water loss through leaves. Your new tree isn't fighting to stay hydrated while also trying to establish roots.
- **Soil is workable.** After a long, dry Boise summer, fall rains soften the ground just enough. The soil is warm from months of sun but no longer baking at the surface.
- **A head start on spring.** A tree planted in October has months of root establishment under its belt before the first flush of spring growth. It wakes up anchored instead of scrambling.

**Think of it this way:** a fall-planted tree spends the winter quietly building its foundation underground. When spring hits, it's ready to grow, not just survive.

By the following June, every single tree had leafed out strong and put on noticeable growth. The maples had new shoots over 18 inches long. The bur oak (notoriously slow to establish) was already pushing new branches.

That's what proper timing does. No magic. Just biology working in your favor.

## Spring Planting in Boise: Your Second-Best Window

Fall isn't always an option. Maybe you just moved in. Maybe the landscaping budget didn't come together until March. That's fine. **Spring is a solid second choice** for tree planting season in Boise.

The window is tighter, though. You're working between two hard boundaries:

1. **After the last hard freeze**, typically mid-April in the Boise area, though it can vary by a week or two depending on the year and your specific neighborhood. North End properties at higher elevation may freeze a bit later than homes down near the river.
2. **Before sustained heat arrives**, once daytime highs consistently hit 85°F and above (usually late June), a newly planted tree is under serious stress.

That gives you roughly **mid-April through late May** as the prime spring planting trees Treasure Valley window. Six weeks. Maybe seven in a mild year.

### Spring Planting Tips for Boise

- **Plant early in the window.** Don't wait for perfect weather. A tree planted April 15 has six more weeks of mild conditions than one planted May 30.
- **Water immediately and consistently.** Spring-planted trees don't have the fall advantage of cool temps and dormancy. They're leafing out and establishing roots simultaneously, and that takes a lot of water.
- **Mulch heavily.** Three to four inches of wood chip mulch in a ring around the base (but not touching the trunk) helps hold moisture in our dry climate.
- **Monitor through the first summer.** A spring-planted tree's first July in Boise is its toughest test. Don't ease up on watering just because it looks established.

## Can You Plant Trees in Summer in Boise? (Yes, but Read This First)

Let's be honest: **summer planting in Boise is risky.** Not impossible, but risky. When daytime temps hit 95°F to 105°F and humidity drops into the single digits, a newly planted tree is fighting for survival.

We see it every year. A homeowner gets excited at a July nursery sale, brings home a beautiful tree, and plants it on a Saturday afternoon. By mid-August, the leaves are scorched, the branches are brittle, and the tree is in serious decline.

### The July Planting Disaster

One homeowner in the Bench neighborhood called us two Augusts ago in a panic. He'd planted five ornamental pears in early July, right before a stretch of eight consecutive days above 100°F. Despite watering every day, three of the five trees were dead by Labor Day. The other two survived but took two full growing seasons to recover.

The cost? Five trees purchased, three trees lost, two replacement trees needed the following fall. He spent nearly double what a single well-timed fall planting would have cost.

**If you absolutely must plant in summer**, here's how to give the tree a fighting chance:

- **Choose container-grown stock only.** Avoid bare-root or balled-and-burlapped trees in summer. Container trees have intact root systems that handle transplant better.
- **Plant in the evening.** Give the tree a full night to settle before the sun hits it.
- **Water deeply every two to three days.** Not a light sprinkle, but a slow soak that sends moisture 12 to 18 inches deep.
- **Consider temporary shade cloth.** For the first two to three weeks, a shade cloth on the south and west sides can reduce heat stress significantly.
- **Skip the fertilizer.** Fertilizing a stressed tree is like asking a marathon runner to sprint at mile 20. The roots can't handle the chemical salts when they're already working overtime to absorb water.

Even with all of this, summer-planted trees in Boise have a notably higher failure rate. Our recommendation? Wait for fall if you can.

## Winter Planting: Why We Almost Always Say No

When to plant trees in Idaho during winter? **In most cases, you don't.**

Boise winters bring frozen ground, and frozen ground means roots can't grow. A tree planted in December or January just sits there, dormant above ground and unable to establish below ground. It's not gaining anything from the experience.

There are rare exceptions:

- **Mild winter days in late February** can work if the ground has thawed and daytime highs are above 45°F for a sustained stretch. But this is really just early spring planting.
- **Container evergreens** are occasionally planted in early December before hard freezes set in, but this is more of a late-fall extension than true winter planting.

For the vast majority of Boise homeowners, **winter is a time to plan, not plant.** Research species. Get your soil tested. [Explore our services](/#services) and line up a spring or fall planting date.

## Boise's Soil and Climate: What Makes Our Planting Different

You can't just follow generic planting advice and expect great results here. Boise has specific conditions that change the game:

### Alkaline Soil (pH 7.2–8.0)

Most of the Treasure Valley sits on alkaline soil. That matters because **certain trees struggle in high-pH soil.** Red maples, pin oaks, and silver maples are notorious for developing iron chlorosis (yellowing leaves with green veins) in our alkaline conditions. The iron is in the soil, but the high pH locks it up so roots can't absorb it.

**Trees that handle Boise's alkaline soil well:**

- Bur oak
- Honeylocust
- Kentucky coffeetree
- Austrian pine
- Rocky Mountain juniper
- Hackberry
- Green ash (where emerald ash borer is not a concern)

### Clay-Heavy Soil

Dig a hole almost anywhere in Boise and you'll hit clay within a foot or two. Clay holds water, sometimes too well. That means:

- **Drainage matters.** Before planting, dig your hole and fill it with water. If it hasn't drained in 24 hours, you have a drainage problem that needs solving before the tree goes in.
- **Amend the backfill.** Mix the native clay soil with compost at roughly a 70/30 ratio. This improves drainage and gives roots an easier medium to push through.
- **Don't create a "bathtub."** If you dig a perfect hole in clay and fill it with fluffy amended soil, water will drain into the hole and sit there. Rough up the sides of the hole with a shovel or pick so water can move laterally into the surrounding clay.

### Low Rainfall and Dry Summers

Boise averages about **12 inches of rain per year.** That's roughly one-third of what trees in the eastern U.S. get. And most of our precipitation falls between November and May, meaning summer-planted trees get almost zero help from Mother Nature.

This is exactly why fall planting in Boise works so well. You're putting the tree in the ground right as the rainy season begins. Nature does some of the watering work for you.

## Species-Specific Timing: Not Every Tree Follows the Same Rules

The general advice (fall first, spring second) holds for most trees. But some species have their own preferences:

### Deciduous Trees

- **Best planted in fall** after leaf drop or in early spring before bud break.
- **Bare-root deciduous trees** (often fruit trees and ornamentals) must be planted while fully dormant, in late fall or very early spring. There is no flexibility here.
- **Container-grown deciduous trees** have the widest planting window, from early spring through mid-fall.

### Evergreen Trees

- **Best planted in early fall** (September) or **mid-spring** (April–May).
- Evergreens need a bit more time to establish before winter because they continue losing moisture through their needles all winter long. An evergreen planted in late October may not have enough root mass to keep up with winter moisture loss.
- **Avoid planting evergreens in summer.** They're even more sensitive to heat stress than deciduous trees during establishment.

### Balled-and-Burlapped (B&B) Trees

- **Heavy and time-sensitive.** B&B trees should be planted within a few days of purchase. The root ball dries out fast in Boise's low humidity.
- **Fall is strongly preferred.** The weight of the root ball makes B&B trees harder to handle, and fall's cooler temps reduce stress during the transition.

## First-Year Care After Planting: The Make-or-Break Period

Planting a tree is just step one. **The first 12 months determine whether your tree thrives or merely survives.** Here's the care schedule we recommend for Boise:

### Watering Schedule

- **Weeks one through four:** Water deeply every two to three days. Each watering should deliver roughly 10 to 15 gallons for a two-inch caliper tree.
- **Months two through six:** Water deeply once a week. Increase to twice weekly during any heat wave above 95°F.
- **Months six through twelve:** Water every 10 to 14 days during active growth. The tree's roots should be spreading into native soil by now.
- **Key rule:** Water slowly. A slow trickle from a garden hose at the base of the tree for 20 to 30 minutes is better than a quick blast from a sprinkler.

### Staking

- **Not every tree needs staking.** If the trunk stands upright on its own and the tree doesn't rock in the wind, skip the stakes.
- **If you do stake**, use two stakes on opposite sides with flexible ties. Never use wire or rigid ties; they'll girdle the trunk as it grows.
- **Remove stakes after one year.** Trees that stay staked too long develop weak trunks because they never learn to support themselves.

### Mulching

- **Three to four inches deep** in a circle extending two to three feet from the trunk.
- **Keep mulch four to six inches away from the trunk itself.** Mulch volcanoes (those mounds piled against the trunk) cause rot, disease, and pest problems.
- **Use wood chips or shredded bark.** Avoid rock mulch near new trees; it radiates heat and doesn't improve the soil.

### What NOT to Do

- **Don't fertilize in the first year.** The tree needs to focus on root growth, not foliage production. Fertilizer pushes top growth at the expense of roots.
- **Don't prune heavily.** Remove only dead, damaged, or crossing branches in year one. The tree needs every leaf it has to produce energy for root development. For ongoing structural pruning after the first year, [professional tree trimming](/blog/tree-trimming-boise/) keeps your trees healthy and well-shaped.
- **Don't let the irrigation system do all the work.** Sprinkler systems water the lawn, not the tree. New trees need targeted deep watering at the root zone.

## Five Common Tree Planting Mistakes in Boise

After 15 years of planting and treating trees across the valley, we see the same mistakes on repeat:

1. **Planting too deep.** The root flare (where the trunk widens at the base) should be visible at or slightly above the soil line. Burying it leads to trunk rot and slow decline. This is the number one planting mistake in America, and Boise is no exception.
2. **Skipping the drainage test.** Boise's clay soil can trap water around roots. Always test drainage before planting.
3. **Choosing the wrong species for alkaline soil.** That beautiful red maple at the nursery will look great for one year, then spend the next decade yellowing and struggling. Match the tree to the soil.
4. **Underwatering in the first summer.** Even fall-planted trees need supplemental water through their first Boise summer. Don't assume they're established just because they survived winter.
5. **Planting too close to the house.** A tree that's cute at six feet will be massive at maturity. Know the mature canopy spread and root zone before you pick your spot. Most shade trees need at least 15 to 20 feet from the foundation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What month is best to plant trees in Boise, Idaho?

**October is the single best month** for tree planting in Boise. Soil is still warm enough for root growth, air temperatures have cooled to reduce transplant stress, and the fall rainy season provides natural moisture. September is also excellent, and mid-April through May serves as the best spring window.

### Can I plant a tree in Boise in July or August?

You can, but expect a significantly higher failure rate. Boise's summer heat, regularly exceeding 100°F, puts extreme stress on newly planted trees. If you must plant in summer, choose container-grown stock, water deeply every two to three days, and consider temporary shade cloth. Waiting until fall is almost always the better choice.

### How do I know if my Boise soil is too alkaline for a specific tree?

A soil test is the most reliable method. The [University of Idaho Extension](https://www.uidaho.edu/extension) offers soil testing services. Most Boise soil ranges from pH 7.2 to 8.0. If your soil is above 7.5, avoid acid-loving species like red maple, pin oak, and blueberry. Stick with alkaline-tolerant species like bur oak, honeylocust, and hackberry.

### How often should I water a newly planted tree in Boise?

For the first month, water deeply every two to three days, roughly 10 to 15 gallons per session for a standard two-inch caliper tree. Reduce to once weekly for months two through six, and every 10 to 14 days after that through the first full year. Increase frequency during heat waves above 95°F. Always water at the base of the tree, not with overhead sprinklers.

### Is it better to plant bare-root or container trees in Boise?

It depends on timing. **Bare-root trees** are cheaper and establish quickly, but they must be planted while fully dormant, in late fall or very early spring. **Container trees** offer more flexibility and can be planted from spring through mid-fall. **Balled-and-burlapped trees** are best for large specimens but should be planted within days of purchase, ideally in fall. For most Boise homeowners, container trees offer the best balance of convenience and success rate.
